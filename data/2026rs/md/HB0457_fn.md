Institutions of Higher Education - Provision of Menstrual Hygiene Products - 
Requirement 
 
 
This bill requires each institution of higher education that provides on-campus housing to 
provide, free of charge, menstrual hygiene products in all student health centers by 
August 1, 2028. The bill takes effect July 1, 2026. 
   
 
Fiscal Summary 
 
State Effect:  Higher education expenditures increase at public four-year institutions of 
higher education that provide on-campus housing, likely minimally, beginning in FY 2029, 
although some costs may be incurred sooner. Revenues are not affected.  
 
Local Effect:  Higher education expenditures increase at local community colleges that 
provide on-campus housing, likely minimally, beginning in FY 2029, although some costs 
may be incurred sooner. Revenues are not affected. This bill may impose a mandate on 
a unit of local government. 
  
Small Business Effect:  None, as discussed below. 
  
 
Analysis 
 
Bill Summary:  “Menstrual hygiene products” is defined as size-appropriate tampons or 
sanitary napkins for use in connection with the menstrual cycle. 
 
Current Law:  Chapters 705 and 706 of 2021 require each local board of education to 
ensure that each public school provide, at no charge to the students, menstrual hygiene 
products via dispensers in the women’s restrooms at the school. A public middle or high 
school must install menstrual hygiene product dispensers in all women’s restrooms. A

Institution of Higher Education 
 
“Institution of higher education” is defined as an institution of postsecondary education 
that generally limits enrollment to graduates of secondary schools and awards degrees at 
either the associate, baccalaureate, or graduate level. It includes public, private nonprofit, 
and for-profit institutions.  
 
State/Local Expenditures:  Each institution of higher education that provides on-campus 
housing must provide menstrual hygiene products free of charge in all student health 
centers, on or before August 1, 2028. Accordingly, beginning in fiscal 2029, higher 
education expenditures increase at University System of Maryland constituent institutions, 
Morgan State University, St. Mary’s College of Maryland, and local community colleges 
that provide on-campus housing (i.e., Allegany College of Maryland and Garrett College). 
Compliance may result in some one-time costs (such as dispenser purchase and 
installation) in addition to the ongoing operating costs related to ensuring an adequate 
supply of products (restocking, monitoring, and possible maintenance). Even so, the 
increase in expenditures for most institutions is likely minimal as some campuses already 
provide menstrual hygiene products free of charge in student health centers, and the 
requirement is limited to these locations rather than all restrooms, which constrains overall 
costs. Actual costs depend on existing practices, implementation choices, and usage levels 
at each institution. 
 
Small Business Effect:  The bill applies only to degree-granting institutions of higher 
education, as defined in § 10-101 of the Education Article, and does not apply to private 
career schools or other non-degree-granting postsecondary programs.  
 
Additional Comments:  The Maryland Independent College and University Association 
advises that requiring free menstrual hygiene products in all campus health centers creates 
modest, ongoing operating costs for universities, including supplies, dispensers (if needed), 
restocking labor, and minimal maintenance. Costs would vary based campus size and usage 
levels. However, overall costs are likely minimal at most institutions.   
 
 
Additional Information 
 
Recent Prior Introductions:  Similar legislation has not been introduced within the last 
three years. 
 
Designated Cross File:  None.
