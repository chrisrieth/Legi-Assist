Behavioral Health Programs - Residents at Inpatient Facilities - Sex Offender 
Registry Compliance 
 
 
This bill requires behavioral health programs that own, lease, operate, or control an 
inpatient facility to take affirmative and reasonable steps to determine whether a resident 
is required to register as a sex offender. Steps must include asking the resident whether 
they are required by law to register, and if so, verifying whether the resident is listed on 
the Maryland Sex Offender Registry. If a program determines that a resident is required to 
register, it must take reasonable steps to ensure compliance, including (1) informing the 
resident of their legal obligations; (2) assisting with coordination or communicating with 
appropriate law enforcement or registry authorities; and (3) documenting compliance 
efforts. A program may demonstrate compliance by maintaining written policies requiring 
inquiry and registry checks and documenting the reasonable steps taken to ensure 
compliance. The bill also establishes penalties for violations of the bill and makes 
conforming changes. 
 
 
Fiscal Summary 
 
State Effect:  General fund expenditures increase by $155,300 in FY 2027. Future years 
reflect annualization, inflation, and ongoing costs. Penalty provisions are not anticipated 
to materially affect State revenues.

Analysis 
 
Bill Summary:  “Inpatient facility” means a dwelling group home, treatment residence, or 
housing unit that is owned, leased, operated, or otherwise controlled by a behavioral health 
program. “Resident” means an individual who resides at an inpatient facility, regardless of 
the individual’s length of stay. 
 
For a first violation of the bill, the Maryland Department of Health (MDH) must issue a 
written notice of deficiency to the program and require the program to submit and 
implement a corrective action plan within a reasonable time specified by MDH. For a 
second violation or a failure to correct a deficiency, the program is subject to a civil penalty 
of up to $5,000 per violation, and MDH may (1) require continued or enhanced corrective 
action and (2) impose enhanced monitoring or reporting requirements. For any subsequent 
violation or a failure to correct a deficiency, the program is subject to a civil penalty of up 
to $10,000 per violation, and MDH may impose licensing or certification sanctions 
otherwise authorized under the Health-General Article, including probation, suspension or 
revocation, or denial of renewal. 
 
The bill may not be construed to (1) require a behavioral health program to register a 
resident with the Maryland Sex Offender Registry on the resident’s behalf; (2) require a 
program to make legal determinations beyond reasonable verification of a resident’s 
registration status; or (3) modify registration obligations, classifications, timelines, or 
penalties under the provisions of State law relating to the Maryland Sex Offender Registry. 
 
Current Law: 
 
Sex Offender Registry 
 
Generally, a person convicted of a sex crime or other specified crime in Maryland, 
including kidnapping and false imprisonment under specified circumstances, is required to 
register with the State sex offender registry upon release from prison or release from court 
if the person did not receive a prison sentence. Offenders who are required to register in 
other states and who come to Maryland are required to register upon entering Maryland. 
Offenders from other states who may not be required to register in the home state are 
required to register in Maryland if the crime would have required registration in Maryland 
if committed in Maryland. 
 
The registry is maintained by the Department of Public Safety and Correctional Services. 
All registrants must initially register with the appropriate supervising authority and/or with

Behavioral Health Programs 
 
Generally, a behavioral health program must be licensed by the Secretary of Health before 
providing program services in the State. 
 
The Behavioral Health Administration (BHA) is required to adopt regulations that include: 
 
 
requirements for licensure of a behavioral health program, including a requirement 
that the program (1) establish and implement a plan for the safety of patients or 
(2) implement a safety or emergency plan established by the program for another 
purpose and revise the safety plan at least every five years; 
 
the process for a program to apply for a license; 
 
a description of programs that are required to be licensed; 
 
requirements for the governance of a behavioral health program, including (1) a 
provision prohibiting a conflict of interest between the interests of a provider and 
patient; (2) a provision authorizing a program licensed as an outpatient mental 
health center to satisfy any regulatory requirement that the medical director be on 
site through the use of telehealth by the director; and (3) a provision authorizing a 
psychiatric nurse practitioner to serve as a medical director of an outpatient mental 
health center, including through telehealth; 
 
provisions for inspections of a program, including inspection and copying of the 
program’s records in accordance with State and federal law; and 
 
provisions for denials, sanctions, suspensions, and revocations of licenses, including 
imposition of civil money penalties and hearings.

coordinators to oversee compliance, respond to complaints, and issue corrective actions, as 
well as one part-time health policy analyst to develop regulations and engage stakeholders. 
Under these assumptions, MDH general fund expenditures increase by $266,322 in  
fiscal 2027, with costs ranging from $236,902 in fiscal 2028 to $270,975 in fiscal 2031.
