Commission on the House of Reformation and Instruction for Colored Children - 
Establishment 
 
 
This bill establishes the Commission on the House of Reformation and Instruction for 
Colored Children (HRICC), to be staffed by the Office of the Attorney General (OAG). 
The purpose of the commission is to (1) research the history, operations, and resident 
deaths at HRICC; (2) provide a complete and public accounting of the children who died 
and were buried on the grounds of HRICC; (3) promote healing and reconciliation through 
truth‑telling, acknowledgment, and recommendations for systemic reform; and (4) ensure 
descendant and community voice throughout the investigation and reconciliation process. 
The commission must submit its initial report and final report to the Governor and 
General Assembly by December 31, 2027, and December 31, 2029, respectively. The bill 
takes effect July 1, 2026, and terminates on June 30, 2030. 
 
 
Fiscal Summary 
 
State Effect:  General fund expenditures increase by $250,000 annually from FY 2027 
through 2029. The FY 2027 budget as introduced includes $250,000 of related funding, as 
discussed below. OAG can staff the commission with existing resources. No assumed 
effect on revenues.

Commission Responsibilities 
 
Among other things, the commission must: 
 
 
direct and oversee the investigation of HRICC by contracting with qualified 
professional experts, including universities, research institutions, and independent 
practitioners, to conduct (1) forensic anthropology, archeological surveys, and 
historical research; (2) location and documentation of all graves; (3) identification 
of individuals buried at the site; (4) reconstruction of institutional conditions, 
policies, and practices throughout the facility’s history; (5) documentation of deaths, 
including circumstances, causes, and State responses; and (6) analysis of systemic 
failures that allowed harms to occur; 
 
when appropriate and approved by the commission, authorize contracted experts to 
conduct exhumation and forensic analysis of remains in accordance with best 
practices in forensic anthropology and bioethics; 
 
direct contracted experts to establish a DNA database for descendant matching and 
pursue genealogical research to locate and notify descendants; 
 
establish protocols for descendant notification, reburial, and culturally appropriate 
treatment of human remains in consultation with contracted experts and community 
stakeholders; 
 
oversee contracted work for restoration and preservation of the cemetery grounds; 
 
make findings regarding institutional failures and State accountability based on 
research conducted by contracted experts; and 
 
develop recommendations for systemic reform, which may include (1) official 
State acknowledgment and formal apology; (2) reparative measures for descendants 
and survivors; (3) reforms to prevent future deaths in custody and improve current 
juvenile justice policies; (4) ongoing commemoration and education initiatives; and 
(5) any other measure the commission deems appropriate to promote healing and 
reconciliation. 
 
Agency Compliance and Legal Exemptions 
 
All State agencies, units of State government, and political subdivisions of the State must 
cooperate and comply with information requests from the commission and its contracted 
researchers and must provide access to facilities, records, and logistical support necessary 
to conduct the investigation. The commission, through OAG, may issue subpoenas for 
documents and testimony relevant to the investigation.

Notwithstanding any other provision of law, the commission and its contractors are 
authorized to conduct archaeological surveys and test excavations and carry out the 
disinterment of human remains on the grounds of HRICC without separate agency permits. 
The commission must consult with the Maryland Historical Trust and the 
Maryland Department of Health (MDH) regarding methods and safety for such actions.  
 
Title 17 of the Criminal Procedure Article, which requires a law enforcement officer to 
submit a sworn affidavit meeting specified criteria for the initiation of a forensic genetic 
genealogical DNA analysis and search, does not apply to a forensic genealogical  
DNA analysis and a search conducted by the commission if (1) the search is conducted 
exclusively for the humanitarian purpose of identifying the deceased and notifying 
descendants; (2) biological samples are not used to screen for medical conditions or traits; 
and (3) all DNA data is destroyed on the conclusion of the investigation. 
 
Notwithstanding § 3-8A-27 of the Courts and Judicial Proceedings Article or the 
Maryland Public Information Act, the commission and the commission’s contractors 
acting in furtherance of their duties must have full and unrestricted access to all sealed or 
otherwise confidential juvenile records, medical records, and administrative files related to 
HRICC. The commission must maintain confidentiality of any living individual mentioned 
in the records and may disclose the names and circumstances of deceased individuals for 
the purposes of the final report of the investigation and memorialization. 
 
Commission Funding, Procurement, and Contracts 
 
Funding for the commission must be as provided in the State budget. OAG must administer 
funding for the commission, including State appropriations, federal grants, and private 
donations. OAG may pursue federal grants and other funding sources to supplement  
State appropriations, subject to commission approval of grant applications.  
 
OAG may procure goods and services on behalf of the commission. However, to the extent 
practicable and consistent with State and federal law, OAG must procure goods and 
services from Maryland institutions and historically Black colleges and universities in the 
State. The execution of a major contract requires approval by vote of the commission.

State Fiscal Effect:  General fund expenditures are assumed to increase by 
$250,000 annually in fiscal 2027 through 2029. Although the bill terminates at the 
conclusion of fiscal 2030, this estimate assumes that the commission’s expenditures are 
incurred through fiscal 2029 and preparation of the final report (due December 31, 2029) 
generally occurs during fiscal 2030 using existing resources at OAG. 
 
The fiscal 2027 budget as introduced includes $250,000 for efforts generally related to the 
HRICC investigation (and is not contingent on the bill). Although the funding is included 
in the allowance for DJS, this analysis assumes it is made available to OAG or the 
commission to fulfill the commission’s responsibilities. While this analysis assumes 
$250,000 annually through fiscal 2029, the Department of Legislative Services notes that 
the exact expenditures required to complete the work required by the bill are unknown and 
depend in part on challenges that may arise during the course of the investigation. 
 
DJS also advises that it has received a $200,000 grant from the African American Heritage 
Preservation Program to restore the Boy’s Village Cemetery in Cheltenham by (1) using 
ground-penetrating radar to identify unmarked burials; (2) through a hired contractor, 
reset and repair extant grave markers; (3) install fencing around the cemetery’s perimeter 
and erect commemorative signage; and (4) in collaboration with the Department of Military 
Families, develop a cemetery conservation plan. This available grant funding may mitigate 
some of the expenditures assumed to be incurred by the commission’s work under the bill. 
Furthermore, the bill authorizes OAG to pursue federal grants and other funding sources; 
any attainment of such funds has not been accounted for in this estimate. 
 
This estimate assumes that the commission will coordinate with State agencies 
(e.g., MDH’s Records Management Office and the Maryland State Archives) to develop 
workload distribution plans to optimize the use of existing personnel without the need for 
additional staff. Any expense reimbursements for commission members or stipends for
