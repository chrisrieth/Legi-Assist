Walter Sondheim Jr. Public Service Internship Scholarship Program - Funding 
 
 
This bill specifies that the Walter Sondheim Jr. Public Service Internship Scholarship 
Program consists of at least three public service intern fellowships:  (1) the Governor’s 
Summer Internship Program; (2) the Sondheim Maryland Nonprofit Leadership Internship 
Program; and (3) the Sondheim Public Service Law Fellows Program. Beginning 
fiscal 2028, the bill requires the Governor to include an appropriation for the program that 
is at least 150% of the appropriation provided for these programs in fiscal 2026. The bill 
takes effect July 1, 2026. 
 
 
Fiscal Summary 
 
State Effect:  Under one set of assumptions, the University of Maryland Baltimore County 
(UMBC) expenditures for the Walter Sondheim Jr. Public Service Internship Scholarship 
Program increase by approximately $175,000 beginning FY 2028. This analysis assumes 
the additional program expenditures are supported in the State budget through additional 
general fund expenditures for UMBC, as discussed below. This bill establishes a 
mandated appropriation beginning in FY 2028.

Analysis 
 
Current Law:  Chapter 490 of 2007 established the Walter Sondheim Jr. Public Service 
Summer Internship Scholarship Program to assist college and graduate students with 
exploring public service career opportunities through summer internships. Chapter 397  
of 2015 expanded the Sondheim scholarship from a summer program to include fall and 
spring internships and renamed it accordingly. Priority for participation in the program 
must be given to eligible students who (1) are residents of the State; (2) have demonstrated 
interest in careers in public service; and (3) assist in providing legal services to low-income 
State residents, social work services to low-income State residents, nursing services in 
nursing shortage areas, or other services in the public or nonprofit sectors in which there is 
a shortage of qualified practitioners for low-income or underserved residents or areas of 
the State. 
 
The Shriver Center at UMBC administers the program and serves as a clearinghouse for 
public and nonprofit entities that wish to hire interns from the program. The program is 
supported with State general fund appropriations to UMBC. Award amounts, as updated 
under Chapter 536 of 2019, must be a minimum of $2,000 and no more than $5,000, subject 
to the availability of funds. 
 
State Expenditures:  UMBC advises that the Walter Sondheim Jr. Public Service 
Internship Scholarship Program received a total appropriation of $350,000 in fiscal 2026. 
Although this funding is embedded within UMBC’s overall State support and is not 
reflected as a separate line item in the State’s operating budget, this analysis assumes that 
amount represents the fiscal 2026 appropriation for purposes of the bill’s mandate. 
Beginning in fiscal 2028, the bill requires that the Governor include in the annual budget 
bill an appropriation for the Program of at least 150% of the fiscal 2026 appropriation. 
Accordingly, under one set of assumptions, the required minimum appropriation beginning 
is $525,000, representing a minimum increase of $175,000. 
 
Since the program does not receive a discrete line item appropriation, the amount 
considered to be the fiscal 2026 appropriation for purposes of the mandate may depend on 
how the State characterizes the embedded funding within UMBC’s broader appropriation. 
Thus, the actual funding required beginning in fiscal 2028 could vary to the extent a 
different fiscal 2026 baseline is identified. 
 
For context, actual fiscal 2026 expenditures for the program totaled approximately 
$326,600, including both administrative costs and funding awarded for the three specified 
fellowship programs, although UMBC reported the appropriation was $350,000. That 
amount excludes approximately $99,300 associated with the Maryland Department of 
Transportation Fellows Program, which is administered separately. For the purposes of this 
estimate, it is assumed that the bill’s mandate is tied to $350,000 UMBC reports was
